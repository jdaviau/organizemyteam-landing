The season is ending, and it's time to show parents and board members where all that money went. A clear, comprehensive end-of-season financial report builds trust, provides transparency, and sets up your successor (or yourself) for success next year.

This guide walks you through creating a professional financial report, with a template you can customize for your team.

## Why End-of-Season Reports Matter

Beyond just "showing your work," a good financial report:

- **Builds trust** with parents who contributed money
- **Demonstrates accountability** to the organization
- **Provides historical data** for future budget planning
- **Protects you** from questions or accusations later
- **Helps transitions** if the treasurer role changes

A treasurer who provides clear financial reports is a treasurer who gets thanked rather than questioned.

## What to Include in Your Report

### 1. Executive Summary

Start with a high-level overview that busy parents can scan in 30 seconds:

- **Total income** for the season
- **Total expenses** for the season
- **Net result** (surplus or deficit)
- **Current balance** in team accounts
- **Key highlights** (major purchases, unexpected costs)

Example:

> *"The 2024-25 Spring Soccer season generated $8,450 in income against $7,890 in expenses, resulting in a $560 surplus. Our current account balance is $1,240. Major expenses included new goals ($1,200) and tournament travel ($2,100)."*

### 2. Income Breakdown

Detail where money came from:

| Source | Budgeted | Actual | Variance |
|--------|----------|--------|----------|
| Registration fees | $5,000 | $5,200 | +$200 |
| Monthly dues | $2,400 | $2,250 | -$150 |
| Fundraising | $1,000 | $800 | -$200 |
| Sponsorships | $500 | $200 | -$300 |
| **Total** | **$8,900** | **$8,450** | **-$450** |

Notes to include:
- Why variances occurred (more/fewer players, fundraiser shortfall, etc.)
- Any outstanding payments still expected
- Changes from the original plan

### 3. Expense Breakdown

Show where money was spent:

| Category | Budgeted | Actual | Variance |
|----------|----------|--------|----------|
| League fees | $1,500 | $1,500 | $0 |
| Field rental | $1,200 | $1,400 | +$200 |
| Equipment | $1,000 | $1,200 | +$200 |
| Uniforms | $800 | $750 | -$50 |
| Tournaments | $2,000 | $2,100 | +$100 |
| Coaching | $500 | $500 | $0 |
| Administrative | $200 | $140 | -$60 |
| Contingency | $500 | $300 | -$200 |
| **Total** | **$7,700** | **$7,890** | **+$190** |

For each significant variance, provide a brief explanation.

### 4. Account Status

Provide current balances and any pending transactions:

- **Checking account**: $1,240
- **Pending deposits**: $0
- **Outstanding checks**: $0
- **Net available**: $1,240

If you have multiple accounts or sub-accounts, break these out separately.

### 5. Outstanding Items

List anything unresolved:

**Amounts owed to the team**:
- Smith family: $75 (final dues, contacted 3x)
- Tournament refund expected: $100

**Amounts the team owes**:
- Coach reimbursement pending: $45

### 6. Budget vs. Actual Analysis

Provide a brief analysis of how the budget performed:

> *"Overall, we stayed within 3% of our total budget. The main variance was field rental, which increased mid-season when our primary field became unavailable. This was offset by coming in under budget on uniforms due to a team discount.*

> *Fundraising fell short of our goal, primarily due to the car wash being rained out. For next season, I recommend budgeting fundraising more conservatively or having a backup event planned."*

### 7. Recommendations for Next Season

Based on this season's experience, suggest adjustments:

1. **Budget field rental higher** to account for potential increases
2. **Collect tournament fees earlier** to avoid cash flow issues
3. **Set fundraising targets lower** or plan backup events
4. **Maintain current dues structure** which proved adequate
5. **Build reserve fund** by carrying forward this year's surplus

### 8. Appendices

Include supporting documentation:

- Bank statements (or summary)
- Receipt summary for major purchases
- Roster of payments by family
- Copy of original budget for reference

## Presentation Tips

### Format for Readability

- Use tables for financial data
- Include charts if helpful (pie chart for expense categories works well)
- Bold key numbers
- Keep explanations concise

### Prepare for Questions

Common questions you might receive:

- "Why did [expense] cost more than expected?"
- "What happened to the fundraising money?"
- "How does this compare to last year?"
- "What's the money being held for?"

Have answers ready, with documentation to support them.

### Choose the Right Venue

Depending on your team's structure:

- **Email distribution**: For routine annual reports
- **Board meeting**: For approval and questions
- **Parent meeting**: For teams wanting more engagement
- **All of the above**: For maximum transparency
